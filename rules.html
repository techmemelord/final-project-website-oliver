How soccer works

The basics of a match

Two teams try to score by moving the ball into the opposing goal without using their hands or arms.

11 players per team
90 minutes in a standard match
2 halves of 45 minutes
1 team with the most goals wins

The field of play

Know the lines

The ball remains in play until it completely crosses a touchline or goal line, on the ground or in the air, or the referee stops play.

  • Penalty area: the goalkeeper may handle the ball here, and certain defending fouls result in a penalty kick.
  • Halfway line: divides the field and matters when deciding whether a player can be offside.
  • Goal area: marks where goal kicks can be taken.

During play

Five rules you will see in every match

  1. A goal must cross the line

    A goal counts only when the whole ball passes over the goal line, between the posts and under the crossbar, without an attacking offence.

  2. Touchline means throw-in

    When the whole ball crosses a touchline, the opposing team restarts play with a two-handed throw-in from where it left the field.

  3. Goal line decides the restart

    If an attacker touches the ball last, play restarts with a goal kick. If a defender touches it last, the attackers receive a corner kick.

  4. Only the goalkeeper handles the ball

    The goalkeeper may use their hands inside their own penalty area. Other players normally play the ball with their feet, head, chest, or legs.

  5. The referee controls the match

    The referee starts and stops play, applies the Laws of the Game, manages added time, and decides whether an offence occurred.

The rule people ask about most

Offside, made simple

Being in an offside position is not automatically an offence.

  1. Check the attacker's position when a teammate plays the ball.
  2. The attacker is in an offside position if they are in the opponents' half and nearer the goal line than both the ball and the second-last opponent.
  3. The player is penalized only if they then become involved in active play or interfere with an opponent.

There is no offside offence directly from a goal kick, throw-in, or corner kick.

Fouls and misconduct

When play becomes unfair or unsafe

Tripping, pushing, kicking, holding, dangerous challenges, and deliberate handball can result in a free kick. If a direct free kick offence is committed by a defender inside their own penalty area, the opposing team receives a penalty kick.

 

Yellow card

A caution for misconduct such as reckless play, delaying a restart, dissent, or repeated offences.

 

Red card

The player is sent off for serious foul play, violent conduct, or another sending-off offence. The team continues with fewer players.
